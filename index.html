About the Study

Evaluating the ORCS4 screening tool in the real world

The ORCS4 is a self-administered online questionnaire that people with shoulder pain complete entirely on their own — no clinician required. Based on their responses, the tool produces a score that indicates whether their shoulder pain is likely to be RCRSP or not.

Rotator cuff–related shoulder pain (RCRSP) is among the most common presentations in musculoskeletal practice, yet there is no diagnostic gold standard. The Online Rotator Cuff related shoulder pain Screening tool (ORCS4) was developed to identify RCRSP without requiring a clinician encounter — enabling earlier triage and more efficient care pathways.

Earlier versions of the ORCS were tested via telehealth and showed strong promise: higher scores were significantly associated with increased probability of RCRSP, and the logistic regression model achieved 76% diagnostic accuracy. The current study is the next critical step — evaluating the refined ORCS4 against face-to-face clinical diagnosis by experienced physiotherapists using the BESS framework as the reference standard.

This is a single-blinded classical diagnostic accuracy study. Participating physiotherapists are blinded to participant screening tool results and document their diagnosis and clinical reasoning in a structured Google Sheets tracker.

Study Design

Classical diagnostic accuracy study with an embedded qualitative component exploring physiotherapist clinical reasoning in shoulder pain diagnosis.

Reference Standard

Face-to-face physiotherapist assessment guided by a modified BESS diagnostic framework — an accepted, NICE-accredited approach to shoulder pain diagnosis.

Index Test

ORCS4 — the fourth and most refined version of the Online Rotator Cuff related shoulder pain Screening tool, updated through consumer feedback and cognitive interviewing.

Ethics & Governance

How It Works

The diagnostic process, step by step

Participating is designed to slot alongside your existing clinical work. Here's what happens from training through to data collection.

Training

You'll complete a 30–60 minute training session covering the diagnostic framework, documentation requirements, and your responsibilities. Training can be attended live or watched as a video in your own time. Practice entries are reviewed before you start seeing study participants.

Recruit participants

Identify eligible patients with shoulder pain in your clinic. You'll receive scripts, poster materials and email templates to guide how you recruit. Patients consent and complete the ORCS4 questionnaire (~10 mins) independently.

Assess & document

Conduct your normal shoulder assessment, blinded to ORCS4 results. Document your diagnosis and clinical reasoning in a structured Google Sheets tracker.

Regular debriefs

After your first 5 assessments and then every 10 assessments (or 4 months), you'll have a short 5-minute check-in (phone call or Zoom) with the lead researcher to troubleshoot any issues, answer questions, and discuss any cases where you had uncertainty in diagnosis.

What's Involved

Your commitment to the study

We've designed participation to minimise additional burden on your clinical workload. Here's a realistic picture of the time involved.

🎓

Initial training (once)

One 30–60 minute session, live or recorded, covering diagnostic criteria, documentation guidance, and study procedures. Includes 3 practice entries reviewed by the research team before you go live.

📋

Documentation per patient

After each shoulder assessment, you'll log your diagnosis and clinical reasoning in the structured Google Sheets tracker. The BESS criteria are broken into individual yes/no columns for RCRSP, making the process systematic and clear.

💬

Regular debriefings

Short Zoom check-ins (5–15 mins) after your first 5 diagnoses, then every 10 assessments or 4 months. These are designed to troubleshoot any issues, provide feedback, and discuss cases where you felt uncertain about diagnosis.

🔬

Optional qualitative arm

If you opt in, you'll complete a short initial survey about your approach to shoulder diagnosis, and one semi-structured interview (20–45 mins) later in the study exploring your clinical reasoning more broadly.
